## Detection Signals

Provenance Guard will use a multi-signal detection approach to classify submitted content. Each signal will capture a distinct property of the text (semantic understanding on one side, structural writing patterns on the other), so their failure modes won't overlap. A single-signal system would have exploitable blind spots; combining independent signals will make the classifier more robust and harder to fool.

---

### Signal 1: LLM Classifier

An LLM will measure and capture the text's semantic meaning, tone, and context in order to produce a confidence score from **0 to 1**, where 0 indicates likely human-authored and 1 indicates likely AI-generated text.

---

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics

Stylometric Heuristics will capture the writing's structural patterns. It will measure statistical properties of the text and combine them into a confidence score from **0 to 1**.

We'll be using the following three heuristics to calculate the confidence score:

- **Sentence length variance:** will capture how uniform or varied the sentence lengths are across the text.
- **Vocabulary richness (type-token ratio):** will capture the ratio of unique words to total words.
- **Punctuation density:** will capture the frequency of formal punctuation marks (commas, semicolons, colons) per word. AI-generated text tends to use these marks more heavily than human writing, so higher density signals AI-like output.

---

### Combining Both Signals

The two signals will be combined via a **weighted average** into a single final confidence score (0 to 1).

A weighted average is appropriate here because both signals will contribute meaningful, independent information, and we want the final score to reflect both rather than letting one gate or override the other. The weights can be tuned empirically. A reasonable baseline for the weights is 60% LLM classifier / 40% stylometric.

The final score will feed directly into the Uncertainty Representation layer, which will determine how the result is communicated to the user.

---

## Uncertainty Representation

Given the final score based on the combination of both signals, we need to map the raw score to a label so that the result can be effectively communicated to the user. We'll be using the following three labels: `High-Confidence Human`, `Uncertain`, and `High-Confidence AI`. The label thresholds are defined and explained below.

---

### Label Thresholds & Reasoning

We'll be using the following thresholds to map the confidence score to a label:

- **High-Confidence Human:** `0.00 to 0.25`
- **Uncertain:** `0.25 to 0.80`
- **High-Confidence AI:** `0.80 to 1.00`

There are a few reasons we chose these thresholds:

1. **Asymmetry:** Falsely flagging human-authored content as AI-generated is more harmful than missing AI-generated content. To reflect this, the thresholds are intentionally asymmetric: the `High-Confidence AI` label requires a stronger signal (score of 0.80 or above) than the `High-Confidence Human` label (0.25 or below). When in doubt, the system will default to `Uncertain` rather than risk a false accusation.
2. **High-Confidence Human (0.00 to 0.25):** A score in this range means both the LLM classifier and the stylometric heuristics agree the text is likely human-authored. The weighted average can only land this low when both signals are pulling in the same direction.
3. **Uncertain (0.25 to 0.80):** A score in this range will indicate that the two signals are contradicting each other, or that the text is too short for either signal to produce reliable results. Rather than forcing a verdict, the system will surface this ambiguity directly to the user.
4. **High-Confidence AI (0.80 to 1.00):** A score in this range means the LLM classifier is confident the text is AI-generated and the stylometric heuristics are in agreement. The high threshold ensures this label is only applied when both signals converge strongly on the same conclusion.

## Transparency Label Design

| Attribution | Transparency Label |
| :--- | :---: |
| `High-Confidence Human` | "This content was most likely written by a human." |
| `Uncertain` | "We weren't able to determine whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI. This can happen when the content is too short or when our analysis returns mixed results." |
| `High-Confidence AI` | "This content shows strong signs of being AI-generated." |

## Appeals Workflow

**Who can appeal:** Any user whose submission was flagged as `High-Confidence AI`.

**What the user will provide:** A written justification for their appeal, and optionally any evidence they believe is relevant.

**What the system will do:** The system will log the appeal alongside the original submission in the database and update the submission's status to `UNDER_REVIEW`.

**What the human reviewer will see:** The original submission, who submitted the appeal, their written justification, and any supporting evidence they provided.

## Anticipated Edge Cases

### Non-Native English Writers

Users writing in English as a second language may produce text with more uniform sentence structures, a narrower vocabulary range, and simpler punctuation patterns than a fluent native writer would. These are the exact same surface features the stylometric heuristics use to detect AI-generated content, which means this group is at a higher risk of false positives. The system will be prone to misclassifying this group not because their writing resembles AI output semantically, but because the heuristics aren't calibrated for their writing style. The LLM classifier may partially offset this if the semantic content reads as clearly human, but the weighted average could still push the score high enough to produce an `Uncertain` or even `High-Confidence AI` label.

### Structured Creative Content

Poetry and other structured creative forms (sonnets, haiku, villanelles, song lyrics) impose deliberate constraints on sentence length, rhythm, and punctuation that have nothing to do with AI generation. A poem with tightly controlled line lengths will register as low sentence length variance. Spare punctuation in verses will look like a flat punctuation pattern. The stylometric heuristics will likely misread these as AI signals, while the LLM classifier may also struggle since AI models are trained on and produce a lot of structured verse.

### AI-Assisted Editing

A user may write a draft themselves and then use an AI tool to clean up grammar, improve word choice, or smooth out phrasing. The resulting content is a hybrid: the ideas and structure are human-authored, but the surface-level writing patterns have been altered by AI. Both signals will struggle here. The stylometric heuristics will likely pick up on the AI's editing fingerprints (more uniform sentence length, polished punctuation), while the LLM classifier may find the semantic content too coherent and well-structured to read as unassisted human writing. The system will probably return `Uncertain` for this type of content, which is the honest answer, but it means AI-assisted editing will be a consistent source of unresolved cases.

## Architecture

### Submission Workflow

```
SUBMISSION FLOW

               +--------------------------+
               |      POST /submit        |
               +-----------+--------------+
                           |
                           | [raw text]
                           |
            +--------------+--------------+
            |                             |
            v                             v
+--------------------------+    +--------------------------+
|         Signal 1         |    |         Signal 2         |
|      LLM Classifier      |    |  Stylometric Heuristics  |
+-----------+--------------+    +-----------+--------------+
            |                             |
            | [signal score]              | [signal score]
            |                             |
            +--------------+--------------+
                           |
                           v
               +--------------------------+
               |    Confidence Scoring    |
               |      (weighted avg)      |
               +-----------+--------------+
                           |
                           | [combined score]
                           |
                           v
               +--------------------------+
               |    Transparency Label    |
               +-----------+--------------+
                           |
                           | [label + combined score]
                           |
                           v
               +--------------------------+
               |        Audit Log         |
               +-----------+--------------+
                           |
                           | [label + combined score]
                           |
                           v
               +--------------------------+
               |         Response         |
               +--------------------------+
```

**Submission Flow:** The raw text is passed to both signals in parallel, each producing a score between 0 and 1. The Confidence Scoring component combines them into a weighted average, which the Transparency Label component maps to a label. The label and score are written to the Audit Log before the response is returned to the user.

---

### Appeal Workflow

```
APPEAL FLOW

+----------------+
|  POST /appeal  |
+-------+--------+
        |
        | [user justification + optional evidence]
        |
        v
+----------------+
| Status Update  |
|  UNDER_REVIEW  |
+-------+--------+
        |
        | [updated record]
        |
        v
+----------------+
|   Audit Log    |
+-------+--------+
        |
        | [confirmation]
        |
        v
+----------------+
|    Response    |
+----------------+
```

**Appeal Flow:** The user's justification and any supporting evidence are submitted via `POST /appeal`. The system updates the submission's status to `UNDER_REVIEW`, logs the appeal alongside the original submission, and returns a confirmation to the user.
